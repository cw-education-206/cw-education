Apala Mandal

If You Can Speak, You Can Sing

“Remember when we’d sing, just for the love of it?” To Noise Making, by Hozier. Most of us would be uncomfortable singing in front of others, or even singing at all. Strong notions of shame, embarrassment and untalentedness mediate our relationship with the activity of singing. Where does this idea—that I can not sing, or I am not good enough of a singer to be singing in front of others—come from? Through my research, I’ve found, as scholars of singing pedagogy before me have, that one of the sources of this anxiousness is the way we have been taught music in schools. My public pedagogy project aims to convey the crux of my research using two methods. I offer a short video version of my paper, covering a bit of the research process and the main findings. I also offer a song that I have written, and performed. In it, I talk about how insecurities about our singing abilities are common, but they shouldn’t stop us from engaging in singing altogether. Singing, after all, is a human tradition, sustained through centuries, linking individual to individual, and community to community. I sing this song to the tune of ‘Ever Since New York’ by Harry Styles, one of the first songs that I found the courage to sing in public.
